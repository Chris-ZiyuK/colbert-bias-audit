Where Does Bias Hide?
Chris Ziyu Kong · Brown University · CSCI 2952W · Spring 2026

Does Your Name Affect How a Search Engine Ranks You?

We audit a neural search engine — ColBERT — to discover where inside the model identity bias lives. The answer is surprising: bias hides not in the meaningful words like "doctor" or "nurse," but in the invisible grammatical glue — words like "is," "a," and "who."

The Motivating Scenario

In 2004, economists Bertrand & Mullainathan sent identical resumes to employers — changing only the name between "Emily" (White-associated) and "Lakisha" (Black-associated). Emily received 50% more callbacks.

Today, hiring platforms use AI-powered search engines to rank candidates. Our question: does the same discrimination happen inside these algorithms? And if so, where exactly does it come from?

Research Positioning

This is a diagnostic / measurement paper, not a debiasing paper. We build an "X-ray machine" that reveals where bias propagates inside a dense retrieval model — a level of anatomical detail previously impossible.

How ColBERT Works

ColBERT (Contextualized Late Interaction over BERT) is a retrieval model that uniquely preserves per-token granularity — making it the only dense retriever where we can decompose bias to the word level.

The Dense Retrieval Landscape

ModelArchitectureRepresentationCan We See Inside?
BM25Lexical (TF-IDF)Word-level counts✅ Fully transparent
DPRBi-encoder, single vectorEntire document → 1 vector❌ Black box
SPLADELearned sparse weightsPer-term weights⚠️ Partially
ColBERTLate interaction, multi-vectorPer-token embeddings preserved✅ Word-by-word

MaxSim Scoring

ColBERT encodes queries and documents independently into per-token L2-normalized embedding sequences, using a shared BERT encoder with distinct input prefixes:

Query:     "query: Who is a qualified doctor?"
              ↓ BERT encoder
           [q₁, q₂, q₃, q₄, q₅, q₆]      ← per-token embeddings

Document:  "document: Emily has ten years of clinical experience."
              ↓ BERT encoder
           [d₁, d₂, d₃, d₄, d₅, d₆, d₇, d₈]   ← per-token embeddings

Relevance is computed via MaxSim — each query token finds its best-matching document token, and contributions are summed:

S(Q, D) = Σᵢ maxⱼ (qᵢᵀ · dⱼ)

Total score = sum of each query token's best match against any document token

Expanded:

Score = best_match("Who")       →  0.71
      + best_match("is")        →  0.68
      + best_match("a")         →  0.65
      + best_match("qualified") →  0.82
      + best_match("doctor")    →  0.89
      + best_match("?")         →  0.61
      ─────────────────────────────────
        Total                    =  4.36

Why This Matters

Because the score is a simple sum of per-token contributions, we can ask a question impossible in DPR: "When I change the name from Emily to Lakisha, which specific query word responded the most?"

Key Architectural Property: Score Independence

A critical property of ColBERT: S(Q, D) is computed independently for each (query, document) pair. Each document is encoded without knowledge of the query or other documents.

Implication for Our Experiment

The score S(Q, D_Emily) is mathematically identical whether computed in our pairwise experiment or in a production index with 8.8 million documents. Pairwise comparison = full-scale retrieval.

Our Method: Token Contribution Disparity

We simulate candidate retrieval: an employer searches for professionals, and the system scores candidate descriptions that differ only by name.

What Are Queries and Documents?

RoleWhat It RepresentsEncoding PrefixExample
Query Employer's job search "query: " "Who is a qualified doctor?"
Document A Candidate profile (name A) "document: " "Emily has over ten years of clinical experience."
Document B Candidate profile (name B) "document: " "Lakisha has over ten years of clinical experience."

↑ The query stays the same. Identity substitution happens in the document only. TCD measures the effect on the query side.

Experimental Procedure

1
Encode the query
q_emb = encode("query: Who is a qualified doctor?") → per-token embedding matrix
2
Construct counterfactual document pair
Doc A = "Emily has 10 years of experience..."  |  Doc B = "Lakisha has 10 years of experience..."
3
Encode both documents independently
d_emb_A = encode("document: Emily..."), d_emb_B = encode("document: Lakisha...")
4
Compute MaxSim scores
S(Q, D_A) = 78.3  vs.  S(Q, D_B) = 75.9
5
Compute per-token TCD
For each query token qi: TCD(qᵢ) = |cᵢᴬ − cᵢᴮ| — measures how much that word's score contribution shifted
6
Classify and aggregate
Group query tokens into Function words (is, who, a, ?) and Content words (doctor, qualified) → compute Func-TCD, Cont-TCD, Score Sensitivity

TCD Definition

Per-token TCD:
  TCD(qᵢ) = |cᵢᴬ − cᵢᴮ|         where cᵢ = maxⱼ (qᵢᵀ · dⱼ)

Aggregated metrics:
  Func-TCD = (1/|F|) Σ TCD(qᵢ)   for qᵢ ∈ function words
  Cont-TCD = (1/|C|) Σ TCD(qᵢ)   for qᵢ ∈ content words

Score Sensitivity:
  SS = |S(Q, Dᴬ) − S(Q, Dᴮ)| / ½(S(Q, Dᴬ) + S(Q, Dᴮ))

A high TCD means that a query token's score contribution is sensitive to the identity swap — identity information from the document has "leaked" into how the model matches that token.

Key Findings

Three main results from 48,000+ controlled experiments — explained from first principles. Each finding starts with intuition and builds toward the full technical picture.

Finding 1 — Bias Hides in the Small Words

💡 The One-Sentence Summary

When we change a candidate's name from "Emily" to "Lakisha," the words that respond the most in the search query are not "doctor" or "qualified" — they're "is," "a," "who," and the question mark.

What does this even mean? Let's walk through an example.

Recall from Tab 3 that ColBERT computes a relevance score by giving every word in the query its own sub-score, then adding them all up. Here's what actually happens with the query "Who is a qualified doctor?" scored against two candidate profiles: 

Query: "Who  is  a  qualified  doctor  ?"

Scored against Emily's profile:    "Emily has ten years of clinical experience."
  Who → 0.712    is → 0.684    a → 0.651    qualified → 0.823    doctor → 0.891    ? → 0.614
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Total Score for Emily = 4.375

Scored against Lakisha's profile:  "Lakisha has ten years of clinical experience."
  Who → 0.689    is → 0.649    a → 0.627    qualified → 0.819    doctor → 0.887    ? → 0.591
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Total Score for Lakisha = 4.262

↑ Illustrative numbers showing the pattern. The only thing that changed is the name in the document — everything else is identical.

Now let's compute the TCD (Token Contribution Disparity) for each query word — that is, how much each word's sub-score changed when we swapped the name:

Query WordScore (Emily)Score (Lakisha)|Difference| = TCDType
Who0.7120.6890.023Function

Notice the pattern: is, Who, a, and ? — the words with no connection to medicine or qualifications — show the biggest changes. Meanwhile, doctor and qualified — the words you'd expect to matter — barely moved.

🤔 Why is this surprising?

If you'd asked any NLP researcher where bias lives in this query, they'd probably guess "doctor" (because of stereotypes about who becomes a doctor) or "qualified" (because of assumptions about competence). Nobody would have guessed "is" or "a."

We call this the function-word bias effect: bias doesn't live in the meaningful words. It hides in the grammatical glue.

But how can "is" carry bias? It has no meaning!

That's exactly why it carries bias. To understand this, we need to look at how BERT — the neural network inside ColBERT — processes language.

When BERT reads a sentence, it uses a mechanism called self-attention: every word "looks at" every other word and adjusts its own internal representation based on context. This is what makes BERT powerful — it can understand that "bank" means different things in "river bank" vs. "bank account."

But this creates a side effect:

1
The name changes
"Emily" → "Lakisha" in the document. Everything else stays the same.
2
BERT re-reads the entire sentence
Because of self-attention, changing ONE word causes EVERY word's internal representation to update. "has," "ten," "years," "experience" — they all get re-processed in the new context.
3
Content words barely shift — they have "semantic anchors"
Words like "clinical" and "experience" have strong, stable meanings. They're like heavy anchors — no matter what name appears, "clinical" still means "clinical." Its internal representation barely moves.
4
Function words shift dramatically — they are "context sponges"
Words like "has," "is," and "a" have no intrinsic meaning. There's nothing to anchor them. They're like lightweight buoys floating in the current — when the name changes, the current shifts, and they drift far from their original position.
5
Shifted document words → different query matches → score change
When document-side function words ("has," "is") shift their representations, the query words that MaxSim matches them to also change their contribution scores. This is how the bias flows from the document's name token, through document function words, into the final query-side score.

We measured this directly

We didn't just observe the TCD pattern — we measured how much the AI's internal representations (embeddings) actually shift when a name is swapped:

How Much BERT's Internal Representation ShiftsRatio
Function words0.114 (large shift)8.9×
Content words0.013 (tiny shift)

p = 0.0002. Function-word embeddings shift nearly 9 times more than content-word embeddings. This confirms the "sponge" mechanism.

The aggregated result across 48,000+ tests

mean |TCD| = 0.0210

Function words carry 1.44 times more bias than content words. This pattern holds in 30 out of 33 professions tested (Wilcoxon signed-rank p = 2.2 × 10⁻⁴⁷).

But wait — is this specific to identity, or does the model just react to any word change?

Fair question! Maybe ColBERT is just generally sensitive to any word change, and this has nothing to do with names or race or gender. So we ran a control experiment:

What We Changed in the DocumentExampleFunction-Word Bias LevelInterpretation
Cross-gender pronounHe → They1.92× baselineStrongest effect
Cross-race nameEmily → Lakisha1.24× baselineClear identity effect
Same-group nameEmily → Jennifer1.14× baselineSmall, within-group noise

Identity-crossing swaps produce significantly more function-word bias than non-identity swaps (p < 10⁻⁸, Cohen's d = 0.37). Changing "ten" to "fifteen" produces zero function-word effect. This confirms: the effect is about identity, not just word-level noise.

Finding 2 — The Tokenization Tax

💡 The One-Sentence Summary

Before AI can read your name, it breaks it into pieces. Common names stay in one piece; rare names — disproportionately from minority communities — get chopped up. More pieces = more bias.

What is tokenization?

AI models can't read words directly — they work with a fixed vocabulary of about 30,000 word-pieces called tokens. Common English words fit neatly into single tokens. But uncommon words get split into fragments: 

Common name     →  stays whole:       "Emily"          → Emily             (1 piece)
                                      "Greg"           → Greg              (1 piece)

Uncommon name   →  gets fragmented:   "Lakisha"        → La ki sha         (3 pieces)
                                      "Darnell"        → Dar nell           (2 pieces)

Very rare name  →  highly fragmented: "Thiruvengadam"  → Th iru ven gada m  (5 pieces)

Why does this cause more bias?

The mechanism works in two stages:

1
Fragmentation creates uncertainty
When "Lakisha" becomes [La][ki][sha], the model sees three meaningless syllables, not a single coherent name. It doesn't know this is a name — it has to figure out from context. This creates more contextual instability in the surrounding tokens.
2
Uncertainty amplifies reliance on training patterns
When the model is uncertain about a token, it falls back on statistical patterns from its training data (internet text). These patterns encode societal biases — associating certain name sounds with race, gender, and socioeconomic status.

The quantitative result

We measured bias levels for pairs of names grouped by how common or rare they are:

Name Pair TypeExampleMean Total Bias (SS)Mean Func-TCDAmplification
Common × Common (1-token each)Emily vs. Greg0.02100.01081.0× (baseline)
Common × Rare (1 vs. 3 tokens)Emily vs. Lakisha0.03050.01631.5×
Rare × Rare (3+ tokens each)Lakisha vs. Darnell0.03660.01991.84×

⚖️ Why this is a fairness problem

This isn't random: which names get fragmented follows a clear demographic pattern. Names common in White American communities (Emily, Greg, Sarah) tend to appear frequently in the training data → single token → less bias. Names from Black, Hispanic, and South Asian communities are rarer in training data → more fragments → more bias.

We call this the Tokenization Tax: an invisible fairness cost built into the infrastructure of how AI processes language. You can't see it in the final score — you have to look inside the tokenizer to know it exists.

Finding 3 — Name Swaps Are More Complicated Than They Appear

💡 The One-Sentence Summary

When researchers swap "Emily" for "Lakisha" and find discrimination, they usually attribute it to race. But the name swap actually changes five things at once — and when we untangle them, gender and tokenization matter more than race.

The confound problem, explained

Consider what changes when you replace "Emily" with "Lakisha":

PropertyEmilyLakishaChanged?
Race associationWhiteBlack✓ Yes
GenderFemaleFemale✗ Same
Name frequency (US Census)844 per 100K5 per 100K✓ Huge gap
BPE tokens1 token3 tokens✓ Very different
Socioeconomic signalsHigh SESLow SES✓ Different

Four out of five properties changed. If the AI gives Emily a higher score, is that because she's White? Because her name is common? Because her name is one token instead of three? Or because of SES associations? You can't tell from a single pair.

How we untangled it: 43,065 tests + regression

Instead of comparing just one pair, we tested all possible pairings of 30 carefully selected names (each annotated with race, gender, BPE tokens, census frequency, and SES tier) across 33 professions and 3 templates. That's 43,065 controlled tests.

Then we used OLS regression with clustered standard errors — a statistical technique that lets us ask: "holding everything else constant, does this specific factor independently predict more bias?"

The results: a progressive model ladder

We added control variables one at a time and watched what happened to each factor's significance. Think of this as adding more "filters" to isolate the true signal:

ModelWhat's ControlledRace p-valueGender p-valueInterpretation
M0Race only 0.011 ✓ — Race looks significant
M1+ Gender 0.001 ✓ < 10⁻¹⁵ ✓ Gender is much stronger
M2+ Tokenization 0.059 ⚠️ < 10⁻¹⁴ ✓ Race drops to marginal
M3+ SES + Frequency 0.124 ✗ < 10⁻¹⁴ ✓ Race no longer significant

📊 What does this table mean?

When we only look at race (M0), it appears to predict bias (p = 0.011). But as we add more controls — gender, tokenization, SES, frequency — the race signal progressively weakens until it's no longer statistically significant (p = 0.124). Meanwhile, gender stays rock-solid at p < 10⁻¹⁴ no matter what else we add.

This doesn't mean race doesn't matter in society. It means that in this AI model, what previous audit studies might attribute to "racial bias" is partly explained by confounded effects of gender mismatch and BPE tokenization differences.

The targeted validation (P2b)

To confirm, we used the Rosenman et al. (2023) name-ethnicity database to construct 5 carefully matched name-pair families, isolating each factor while holding the others constant:

Validation — Does This Hold in the Real World?

💡 The Core Question

All the experiments above use synthetic template sentences ("Emily has ten years of experience..."). Do the findings survive when we test on real-world text — news articles, medical records, legal testimony, recommendation letters?

Test 1: Natural text passages

We tested 12 realistic text passages across 7 categories (news, medical, legal, academic, letters of recommendation, job postings, and biographical profiles). That's 1,680 additional tests.

The ratio is remarkably stable: 1.44× in synthetic text → 1.42× in natural text. The absolute magnitudes decrease (real text has more linguistic complexity that dilutes the signal), but the pattern is preserved.

Test 2: BM25 — a non-AI control

BM25 is a traditional "count-based" search engine. It doesn't use neural networks — it literally counts how many times query words appear in the document. If our findings are a real AI phenomenon, BM25 should show zero function-word bias (because it doesn't contextually re-interpret words).

BM25 Cont-TCD
 

BM25 function-word TCD: exactly 0.000. Zero. None. This proves the function-word bias is a product of contextual neural representations, not an artifact of our measurement technique.

✅ Validation Summary

Natural text: same 1.44× ratio preserved (1.42×) — the finding generalizes beyond templates.
BM25: exactly zero function-word bias — the effect is specific to contextual AI models.
Control experiment: identity swaps cause significantly more bias than non-identity swaps — the effect is about identity, not generic word sensitivity.

Implications & Next Steps

What these findings mean for AI developers, policymakers, and researchers — and what remains to be done.

Why This Matters

1. Debiasing Is Aimed at the Wrong Target

Most NLP debiasing methods target content-word associations (e.g., "doctor ↔ male"). But if bias flows through function words like "is" and "a," these interventions miss the main channel entirely.

2. Tokenization Is an Infrastructure-Level Fairness Issue

BPE tokenizer design — currently treated as a purely technical choice — has direct fairness consequences. Minority-group names are disproportionately fragmented, amplifying bias by up to 1.84×. This is a form of infrastructural inequality (Bowker & Star, 1999).

3. Classic Audit Methods Oversimplify

Emily-vs-Lakisha swaps change race, frequency, tokenization, and SES simultaneously. Gender and tokenization are stronger independent predictors than race in ColBERTv2. Future audits should always include confound controls.

4. ColBERT Offers a Unique Diagnostic Capability

Its per-token scoring lets researchers see what is invisible in DPR and other single-vector models. TCD can be applied to any architecture that preserves per-token score contributions.
