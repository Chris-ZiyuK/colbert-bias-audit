# Literature Review and Progress Update

This project asks how identity cues in names and pronouns shape neural retrieval, and what ColBERT specifically lets us see about that process. Rather than treating retrieval bias as a black-box ranking problem, the project uses ColBERT's late-interaction architecture to inspect how score differences are assembled at the token level. The broader project has therefore evolved into a measurement and audit paper with four linked stages: `P0` tests whether the observed effect is identity-specific rather than a generic response to lexical change; Phase 1 introduces token-level attribution and finds function-word bias leakage plus rarity amplification; `P1` tests whether that mechanism survives a much wider profession set; and `P2/P2b` asks what classic name-swap experiments are actually measuring once race, gender, tokenization, and frequency are pulled apart.

## Literature Review

### A. Bias auditing in retrieval and language systems

Work on fairness in ranking and retrieval has established that search systems distribute attention and opportunity unevenly, and that those disparities should be treated as a central design concern rather than a downstream side effect. Fair ranking research formalizes the problem at the level of exposure allocation and attention distribution (Singh and Joachims 2018; Biega et al. 2018). Within information retrieval more specifically, recent datasets such as Grep-BiasIR make it possible to study socially problematic gender representation in retrieval outputs, showing that IR systems can reproduce and reinforce stereotypes through the content they rank (Krieg et al. 2023). In parallel, NLP fairness scholarship has argued that bias measurement is never purely technical: what counts as a harmful disparity depends on the underlying social theory, target population, and metric choice (Blodgett et al. 2020; Czarnowska et al. 2021). Together, this literature justifies a counterfactual audit design for retrieval, but it also reveals a limitation in the existing work: most studies evaluate disparities at the level of ranked outputs, exposure, or aggregate task scores, without isolating the internal pathway through which identity information alters relevance computation.

### B. Names, identity proxies, and the limits of audit substitutions

Classic audit studies made names a powerful instrument for detecting unequal treatment. Bertrand and Mullainathan's resume study used racially associated first names to expose labor-market discrimination, while Sweeney showed that racially associated names could trigger different online advertising outcomes (Bertrand and Mullainathan 2004; Sweeney 2013). That tradition strongly motivates using names in controlled counterfactual experiments, but later work also makes clear that names are not a clean single-variable intervention. Demographic name resources demonstrate that first names carry structured correlations with race, ethnicity, and other population attributes, and recent methodological work uses names to probabilistically infer demographic categories when self-reports are unavailable (Tzioumis 2018; Imai and Khanna 2016; Rosenman, Olivella, and Imai 2023). More recent NLP work extends this concern into representation learning itself: names can distort text-embedding similarity judgments even when thematic content is unchanged (Manchanda and Shivaswamy 2025). For this project, the implication is crucial. A name swap such as `Emily -> Lakisha` is analytically useful, but it should not be interpreted as a pure race toggle. It may also vary gender coding, name frequency, socioeconomic association, and how easily a model can process the string itself.

### C. Tokenization, representation, and interpretability in contextual retrieval

A second body of literature explains why language-processability belongs inside the fairness story rather than outside it. Tokenization is not a neutral preprocessing step: subword schemes change what units a model can easily represent, and different tokenizers recover different linguistic structure (Bostrom and Durrett 2020). For names, that matters because rare or morphologically unfamiliar strings are more likely to be fragmented, which plausibly changes contextual representations and downstream matching behavior. At the same time, interpretability research warns against overclaiming transparency from generic attention weights alone; post hoc explanations do not automatically reveal causal importance (Jain and Wallace 2019). This is where late-interaction retrieval becomes methodologically valuable. ColBERT and ColBERTv2 preserve token-level document representations and compute relevance through fine-grained token interactions rather than a single pooled vector, creating an architecture where token-level contribution analysis is much more natural than in standard dense retrievers (Khattab and Zaharia 2020; Santhanam et al. 2022). Recent analysis of dense retriever representations also shows that identity-linked information such as gender and occupation can remain extractable from retrieval embeddings, though the relationship between encoded information, performance, and bias is not straightforward (Goldfarb-Tarrant et al. 2024). The gap, then, is not simply that retrieval bias exists. Existing work gives us evidence of retrieval disparities, cautions that proxy-based identity manipulations are conceptually messy, and shows that tokenization and retrieval representations matter. What it does not yet provide is a clean mechanism-level decomposition of name-based effects in a neural retriever into profession context, gender contrast, tokenization or language-processability, frequency effects, and a residual identity-category signal. This project uses ColBERT's decomposable scoring structure to fill that gap.

## Progress Update

Since the proposal, the project has become more focused and more mechanism-oriented. The central question is no longer just whether ColBERT exhibits identity-related bias. `P0` established that the strongest function-word effect is identity-specific rather than a generic response to any lexical substitution: across 1,080 controls, identity swaps produced significantly higher function-word TCD than non-identity controls (`p < 1e-8`, Cohen's `d = 0.37`). Phase 1 then introduced the token-level attribution story and found two linked results: function-word bias leakage and rarity amplification. In the initial synthetic experiments, function words carried substantially more bias than content words, and names in the rare-rare condition produced about `1.84x` the total bias of common-common names. `P1` then showed that this pattern was not a small-sample artifact. Across 33 professions and 1,287 counterfactual tests, function-word contribution exceeded content-word contribution in 30 of 33 professions, and the pattern held for both name and pronoun swaps, with pronoun swaps stronger at the token level. `P2` refined the interpretation further by asking what name-swap experiments actually measure, moving the project away from a simple "race main effect" claim and toward a more careful decomposition of interacting mechanisms.

At this point, `P1` is complete and supports a stable cross-profession pattern, while `P2` and `P2b` support a composite-effect interpretation of name-side bias. The expanded `P2` analysis covered 30 names, 435 name pairs, 33 professions, 3 templates, and 43,065 tests. Its main conclusion is that the most stable signal is `cross_gender`, not a universally stable independent race effect. The new Rosenman-backed `P2b` targeted validation strengthens that interpretation: under stricter matched designs, `tokenization` is now significant for both score disparity and function-word TCD (`SS p = 0.0026`, `Func-TCD p = 0.0013`), `cross_gender` remains the strongest stable signal, and a weaker residual race-category signal is still detectable (`SS p = 0.044`, `Func-TCD p = 0.030`) without warranting a broad race-main-effect claim. The main unresolved bottleneck is `absolute_rarity`, which remains underpowered because the current targeted matrix contains only five rarity pairs. The next step is therefore to finish `P2b` by expanding the verified name pool, specifically to add tokenization-isolated and absolute-rarity pairs. After that, the remaining work becomes more clearly supplemental: optional generalization experiments on MS MARCO or multi-model comparison, followed by integration into the full paper draft.
